# Evidence Philosophy

## Why provenance matters

A belief is more useful when the system can explain how it came to hold that belief. Provenance preserves accountability and makes the system easier to trust.

## Why confidence is not certainty

Confidence describes the strength of the available support. It does not mean the system has reached certainty. A high-confidence inference is still an inference.

## Why evidence expires

Evidence becomes less useful as time passes. Observations can grow stale, dependencies can change, and once-current facts may become outdated.

## Why verification differs from inference

Inference is a reasoning step. Verification is a confirmation that the current state actually holds. These should remain distinct.

## Why explicit corrections matter

When a new fact supersedes an older one, that change should be explicit. Silent overwrite weakens the system’s reliability and makes contradictions harder to understand.

## Why unknown is preferable to false certainty

The system is stronger when it says, “I do not know yet,” than when it pretends certainty without support.
